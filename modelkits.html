What are Model Kits?


Model kits are actually pretty similar to action figures, in that most have articulated joints. The main difference is that you actually have to build model kits yourself!

Kits come with an assortment of individual parts that you have to take the time to cut out and put together.

Think of it like a more complex Lego set. You're given instructions on how to put everything together, and once you're done you'll have a fully functioning figurine!

The DIY aspect of model kits actually allows for, depending on the size of the kit, a more affordable alternative than action figures of specific characters.

How Model Kits Differ

The world of model kits is actually quite a complex one. There is quite a huge variety of kits you can buy, but I'll simply go over the most common ones:

  1. Character model kits

  2. Gundam/Mech model kits

  3. Object model kits

The first option is my personal favorite, but it is simply a model kit type where you build a model of any available character. You more commonly see this with either anime characters or characters similar to the Mega Man style. Usually pretty affordable since they aren't very big, a good choice if you're willing to put in the effort.

The second choice is a very popular option for a lot of people, model kits where you get to build your own mech. These are usually time consuming and tedious to make, but they have such a high level of detail that it makes for a very imposing collection. These can have a very big range in prices, but most of the nice ones are on the expensive side of things.

The third option is a very niche type, but there are plenty of people who love to build model kits of specific car and aircraft models. It's for a very specific audience, but if that's what you've been wanting to collect then they are actually very affordable. Very few go above the $50 range unless it's much higher end.

It should also be noted that model kits have two main build types; snap on or self-serving. Snap on kits have pieces that are ready to just snap together as soon as their cut out, while the self-serving kits need to be glued individually.
